History of the Android Mascot

It's possible that you know that Android operating system, maintaned by Google it's one of the mostly used mobile devices on the entire world. But maybe you don't know that this little mascot have a name and a curious history? Follow ahead on thiis article to know more about this little robot.

THE FIRST VERSION

The first attempt to create a mascot came in 2007 from a developer named Dan Morrill. He recounts that he opened Inkscape (free software for vectorizing images) and created his own robot version. The goal was simply to personify the system for his team; there was no request from the company to create a mascot.

This first bizarre version was even named in honor of its creator: they would be called the Dandroids.

APPEARS A NEW MASCOT

The idea of ​​having a mascot matured, and the mission was passed on to a professional in the field. Russian illustrator Irina Blok, also a Google employee, was given the task of representing the little robot in a more appealing way.

Irina's main idea was to represent everything graphically with few lines and in a flatter style. The design should also generate quick identification with the viewer. Thus, Bugdroid, the new Android mascot, was born.

The main inspiration for the new Bugdroid's design came from those little figures that illustrate bathroom doors to indicate the gender of each stall. Legend has it that the artist was creating at her desk in the Google office and looked towards the bathrooms, and the connection was immediate: simple, clean, objective.

Another interesting fact about Android is that each version has always been named after a type of candy, in alphabetical order from version 1.5 to 9.0.

  • 1.5 - Cupcake
  • 1.6 - Donut
  • 2.0/2.1 - Eclair
  • 2.2 - Froyo
  • 2.3 - Gingerbread
  • 3.0/3.1/3.2 - Honeycomb
  • 4.0 - Ice Cream Sandwich
  • 4.1/4.2/4.3 - Jelly Bean
  • 4.4 - KitKat
  • 5.0/5.1 - Lollipop
  • 6.0 - Marshmallow
  • 7.0/7.1 - Nougat
  • 8.0/8.1 - Oreo
  • 9.0 - Pie

Unfortunately, Android Q never existed, as Google decided to end this fun practice and started using numbering, which gave rise to Android 10.
